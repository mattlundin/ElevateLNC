3 Reasons Clinicians Should Avoid Using Abbreviations | Tips From a Legal Nurse Consultant

August 1, 2022

As a clinician, you and your resources are stretched thin. To save time it would make sense to abbreviate your documentation – less time documenting more time providing care. What may appear a convenience can have strong legal implications in the long run. This article will review three of the reasons why a clinician should think twice before using abbreviations in their documentation, from the view of a Legal Nurse Consultant.



Three Reasons to Avoid Using Abbreviations

Listen, we get it. As a clinician, you are short on time and at times even shorter on resources. You understand the importance of accurate documentation (link to other blog post) on patient care and still want to make sure you're providing care not just writing about it. The appeal to abbreviate is understandable, but not advised. What may seem like a shortcut in the interim, is not a great practice for the long term.


Let’s review three of the reasons a clinician should avoid using abbreviations in their documentation.


1. Abbreviations can create mystery. When a provider has to decode an acronym, that can take time away from patient care. Mystery can create a lapse in care, which can have devastating effects on the patient. Sometimes what may make sense to you at the moment may not translate to another provider. For example, you are a nurse caring for a patient, and the provider writes, “Patient may ambulate AFAT.” Would you understand what that meant? How much time would it take to figure out the acronym? The provider is unavailable via phone, so you enlist the help of another nurse. Now, two nurses are consumed trying to figure out an order and distracting from patient care. (Ps: it means as far as tolerated, and no, it is not an approved acronym)



2. Abbreviations can be misinterpreted. Abbreviations are not detrimental, but the consequences could be deadly when used incorrectly or misunderstood. One abbreviation could signify up to four different medical terms. For example, MS can indicate morphine sulfate, multiple sclerosis, mental status, or mitral stenosis. How can the reader understand what is intended by two standalone letters? It is safer to spell out the word or words rather than risk an unintended interpretation.



3. “Abbreviations can cause legal nightmares.” As Nursing Services Organization (NSO), a leader in providing Nursing insurance services and education, so aptly put it, abbreviations can be a legal nightmare. Anytime you document on a patient chart, it is considered evergreen – meaning it should be understood in the moment as well as in the future. It may be tempting to get creative and save some time in the moment, but deviation from an approved list can have legal implications. Can you prove what you intended to document with an abbreviation 5 years prior if called to testify? Can you conclusively defend your intention?
